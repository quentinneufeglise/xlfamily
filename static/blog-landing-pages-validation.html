3 Reasons Why Landing Pages Are An Effective Tool For Idea Validation

  • by Aneliya Evtimova

    Digital Analyst

NB: the landing page we are referring to here, in inbound marketing, may not be the same landing page term that your design and/or development team may be referring to. So it’s important for all of us to be on the same page here (pun intended :) ).

You did it! You have worked so hard – from the ideation process, to hundreds of meetings and possibly arguments here and there with your colleagues, but in the end it was all well worth it – your idea is polished and now you can’t wait to validate it through a prototype. Except, you are not quite sure what would be the best approach to do this.

Try Landing Pages.

What is a Landing Page?

Landing Pages are standalone pages. Usually not connected to your website, they still have the look and feel of your brand but they are promotion-specific. There is one primary action that your visitors can undertake such as to sign up for a form, buy your product, etc.

Landing Pages and prototyping

To validate your prototype though a landing page, you should design it in a way that you can use the one primary action. For example, if you want to validate the need for a fitness app that you want to create, you’d want to focus on how many pre-sign ups occur within a certain period of time. Your landing page will then be focused namely on this – explaining the app and asking for the users to sign up for the prelaunch.

My favorite 3 reasons to use landing pages are:

1. Low resources requirements vs a big payout

You don’t have to be a developer or a designer to create and publish your landing page – there are amazing tools that you can use for free (during a tryout period) or by creating a paid account. Unbounce, Leadpages and Instapage are to name a few. Creating your landing page can be as easy as making a PowerPoint presentation (of course, provided that you have the content and strategy set in advance).

2. Easy way to structurally present the idea

The structure of the landing page is predisposing you to provide a concise and sharp explanation about your idea, which will work only to your advantage. Think of it as a one-pager summary that your customers can evaluate.

3. Quantifiable results through analytics

Because there is only one action that you will be asking your users to do, you will have 1 primary metric to care about – conversion rate – the ratio of people who did the action versus all the people that were on your page. Bam! Clear. They are either interested or they are not. And if you are as much as a data-freak like I am, you may want to extend your analysis and want to know more about the juicy details – how long did it take them to click on that call to action, where exactly are they based, are they male or female, etc. Whether you decide to go the extra mile or not, you will have a clear indication of the interest in your prototype.

So, don't waste any more time – write your compelling text, explain your idea well, publish your landing page, and wait for your verdict from their majesties - the customers. Good luck! :)
