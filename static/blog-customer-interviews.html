Customer Interviews: Don't Let People Lie To You

  • by Anne Twilt

    Innovation Consultant

Validation. It sounds easy enough... You have been working on your idea for weeks and you know it like the back of your hand. What you need to do now is go outside, find some people who belong to your target group, describe to them your concept and see how they react. No sweat, right?

Well actually, you’re wrong. There is an entire science behind how to conduct customer interviews. One reason why it isn't as simple as it sounds is because often the way you phrase your questions influences the answers you get, hence providing you with biased information.

It is hard to collect unbiased data. This is a well-known fact, heavily emphasized by numerous academics. And still, many of us tend to conveniently forget the rules of science when we start validating our innovation projects.

During a workshop I participated in some time ago, I learned about The Mom Test: a book about customer interviews and how to circumvent people from (unintentionally) lying to you. It is a small and comprehensive book, which I would recommend to anyone who wants to get busy with customer interviews. Here are a few tips I have found most helpful that you might want to keep in mind for next time (I know I would):

  • An interview of 15 minutes should be enough to single out whether there is a real problem or need.
  • Make sure to keep your ego out of the conversation. You are most probably very passionate about the idea and convinced that it's great. That doesn't matter now. Don't make the person in front of you feel like you are the idea. If you show your baby to the world, people are not going to tell you it is ugly. They will lie to you to avoid a conversation from becoming awkward.
  • Do not start pitching your solution. Keep it more casual, dig for the problem and see if it’s really an issue your target group can relate to.
  • Ask questions and genuinely listen. Do not only pay attention to the things you want to hear. You can discover a lot more useful information that you didn't even know to look for.
  • Use the 5 Whys technique to get to the core of a need, problem or situation. This technique is remarkably simple: when a problem occurs, you uncover its nature and source by asking "why" no fewer than five times. Although it might feel awkward at first, you'll find that it works when you try it.
  • Don't ask "would you do X" or “what would you pay for Y". It's easy for people to say yes, even if in reality they would never. Ask for factual behavior: "When did you last do X?" "Can you run me through an example of a situation in which you struggled with Y. How did you solve it?"
  • Ignore compliments (that’s such a great idea; I love it; you are amazing; etc.) and search for forms of actual commitment. This doesn't necessarily have to be a financial commitment. Other forms of commitment can include time (a follow-up meeting or some hours of help) or access to a connection ("you should talk to Pieter, let me introduce you to him").
  • Keep in mind that invalidation can save you (and potentially the organization you work for) lots of effort and money. Do not turn a deaf ear to negative feedback.

Remember that validation can take place anywhere. You can ask questions at your aunt's birthday party or while you're having a beer at the local bar. Be genuinely interested and ask a lot of whys. You can learn a lot about your target group interests and problems!

Doing this can be tricky. Finding the sweet spot of innovation that resonates with your organization’s identity, message and vision for the future is actually more work than one might expect. Whether it is one or more focus areas that you are going for, you’ll find your innovation hotspots at the intersection of current/arising trends and your corporate strategy.

In other words, it’s about finding the connection between two worlds – the organization’s and the one that surrounds it.

That calls for a number of activities such as trend-watching and analysis of the organizational situation in the industry specific context. But also for conveying organizational self-reflection, a.k.a. iSWOT analysis, to pin-point strengths and weaknesses that should unarguably be taken into account when looking for new business opportunities. Finally, reflecting on accumulated learnings to make actionable plans for going further with innovation.

Clearly, the above stated is merely an outline of what is the first and defining step in getting on with corporate innovation. And if you can say you’ve checked out step two, too, tighten your seat belt because you’re only getting ready for your journey down the innovation funnel. Stay tuned:-)
