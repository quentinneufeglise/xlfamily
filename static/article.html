Get inspired by everyday life: the art of copycatting

  • by Jeske Eenink
     Innovation Consultant

One of the reasons I love working in innovation is that it makes you look at the world differently. One can find inspiration from all kinds of experiences, even while shopping. Zara is one of my favorite shops; and with 450 million items sold yearly they are thriving. However, they shamelessly copy the collections of high-end fashion brands such as Yeezy and less known indie brands. This makes you wonder: how do copying and innovation go hand-in-hand?

Copying and innovation kinda like each other. Actually, they are in love. And their lovechild is called Copycat. A copycatter understands that copying lies at the heart of creativity. By copying something I mean just about anything - the full product, the service or just the successful elements. Once you can copy something, and you understand it, you could figure out how to make it better.

Why would you go through all the effort and take all the risks to become a first-mover, if you can just sit back, relax, let somebody else do the heavy lifting and just put the effort in once it is ready? Come on. Be real. Everybody gets his inspiration somewhere. Take Instagram for example, that recently implemented Snapchat-like features into their app.

The same goes up for fashion, there is more to it than just having jaw-breaking, hip designs. Creativity is just the start of innovation. Bringing your idea to the right market at the right time is key. So instead of focusing on design, like everybody else in the fashion business, Zara copied the lean way of working, originally implemented in the car business. With their in-house designers and production, they managed to create a three-week supply-chain that delivers the catwalk almost directly to your closet.

So yes, copying and innovation go hand-in-hand. Very closely so. Innovation is not always about creating something completely new, but about seeing opportunities and making the most of them. 97,8% of the value of innovations goes to imitators. So why not structurally implement copying in your innovation strategy?

During innovation projects we challenge our customers at XL family with the controversy of copying success factors versus creating something on your own. What is your strategic focus? Where can you find your opportunities? Can you source inspiration from other success stories? Go and find your business.
