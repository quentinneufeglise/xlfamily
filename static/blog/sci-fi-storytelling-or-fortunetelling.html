Sci-Fi: Storytelling Or Fortunetelling

  • by Filip Lacor

    Innovation Consultant

As humankind, we have always been attracted to the unknown and the mystical. We are explorers – that’s in our nature. We explore the depths of our oceans, the heights of our mountains. Outer space exploration, looking towards the stars and finding different, distant worlds has been a particular fascination of ours.

In one way or another we are all addicted to the future and love speculating on what it would behold. That’s how science fiction came to be. From new gadgets and robots to new worlds and space travel – it’s coming out of the books into movies and in recent years/decades into our daily reality.

Think about Le Voyage dans la Lune (1902), which is considered as the first science fiction film ever made. It tells a story about a spacecraft being launched to the Moon in a large cannon. Of course, it didn’t happen exactly so but the plan and execution for getting to the Moon was a success (not paying attention to the conspiracy theories!).

How about famous examples of James Bond and his collection of neat gadgets. We all want one of these underwater breathing devices we saw him use in Thunderball (back in 1965!). It was quite exciting to find a comparable product been backed with $895,000 through an Indiegogo crowdfunding campaign. Unfortunately, recently the product was found to scientifically impossible.

On the other hand, we have success stories, like showcase driverless cars, facial recognition technologies, jetpacks, and the most famous gesture-based user interfaces, predicted in the movie Minority Report (2002). Lots of these technologies are currently being developed or even already realized!

More recent movies include Her (2013) and Ex-Machina (2015), where we get introduced to near-future scenarios in which humanoid robots and operational systems with artificial intelligence are adapting, evolving and learning fast. And though these still seem as a part of the somewhat distant tomorrow, they are bringing up important existential questions and even concerns about the way we love, live and interact.

Saying all this, I can’t help but think that science fiction is more than just storytelling, it is a sort of fortunetelling. The few examples above are just touching the top of the iceberg of predictions that already came or about to come true. But one thing is for sure – we have the capacity to build anything we put our minds to. Sometimes it’s a matter of years, sometimes of decades, but what science fiction may eventually turn into is a window to the future.
